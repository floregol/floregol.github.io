Active Learning for Humans

When teaching something new to someone, we often have the intuition that with the right examples, the person will learn faster.

In machine learning, finding the best points to train an algorithm is called Active Learning (AL).

Given some data $\mathcal{D}$ and model knowledge $ f_{\theta} = ML(\mathcal{D})$, the goal of AL is to find the best datapoints $\mathcal{D}^*_{\mathcal{L}} \in \mathcal{D} $ that will maximize the performance of $f_{\theta} = ML(\mathcal{D}^*_{\mathcal{L}})$.

Sometimes, ML and humans are called to solve the same tasks:

For example, both medical students and algorithms learn to distinguish cancerous and benign images. Hence, we can easily hypothesize that their are helpful points for humans $\mathcal{D}^*_{\mathcal{L}} \in \mathcal{D} $ , and helpful points for algorithms $\mathcal{D}^*_{\mathcal{L}} \in \mathcal{D} $ .
